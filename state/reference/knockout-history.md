### 1.6 Second-half comeback rate

_Defined as: team trailing (not drawing) at the 80-minute mark that goes on to force extra time or win the match outright in regulation._

Notable second-half knockouts comebacks in the sample:
- WC 2018 R16: Belgium 0-2 Japan at 52', Belgium won 3-2 (comeback)
- WC 2022 QF: Netherlands 0-2 Argentina at ~73', Netherlands pulled back to 2-2 (forced ET); then Argentina won PSO (partial comeback — did not advance but did equalise)
- WC 2022 Final: France 0-2 at 80', came back to 2-2 after 90' then 3-3 in ET (comeback — did not advance)
- Euro 2020 R16: Croatia 0-3 Spain at 57', Croatia pulled back to 3-3 (Spain won in ET 5-3)
- Euro 2024 R16: England 0-1 Slovakia in 90+5', Bellingham equalised, then won in ET

Full second-half comebacks (trailed at ~80', ultimately advanced or equalised to force ET): approximately 4-6 instances in 90 relevant matches = **5–7% comeback-and-advance rate per match**. However, the more useful base rate is: of teams trailing in the second half, roughly **10–15% manage to equalise or overturn the deficit before full-time or in ET** — this is the structural "underpriced draw" signal for the knockouts.

_Note: Precise 80-minute trailing statistics are not available from the Wikipedia-level sources. The above is directional based on notable instances. Source: Wikipedia match reports for all 6 tournaments; Opta Analyst WC/Euro shootout facts pages; checked 2026-06-27._

### 1.7 Penalty shootout base conversion rate

**Euro PSO conversion:** European Championship Penalty Shootouts: The Facts (Opta Analyst, 2024) — broadly similar at approximately 70–72%.

**By penalty order (WC data, Opta Analyst):**
- Kicks 1–3: >71% conversion
- Kicks 4–5: 64–67% conversion
- Kick 6+: ~50–64% conversion (pressure accumulation)

**Key finding:** Shootout conversion is ~10 percentage points lower than in-play penalties (79% in-play vs 69% PSO). Psychological pressure is the documented driver.

**Team-shooting-first advantage:** 17 WC PSOs won by first-shooting team, 18 by second. No significant advantage overall. Recent quirk (2022 onwards): 9 of last 11 WC PSOs won by side shooting second (small sample, may not persist). Source: Opta Analyst; checked 2026-06-27.

### 1.8 R32 base rates — analogized from Euro R16

**IMPORTANT: The WC 2026 Round of 32 has no historical precedent.** The 48-team format is new. The R32 is the first knockout round with 32 surviving teams.

The closest structural analogue is the **24-team UEFA Euro Round of 16** (Euros 2016, 2020, 2024), where 16 teams met in a knockout bracket after a group stage. The Euro R16 involves a similar mix of quality-tier compression as a 48-team WC R32 would: many matches between a group winner and a third-place qualifier or strong runner-up, with meaningful quality gaps.

**Euro R16 base rates (24 matches, 3 tournaments):**

| Metric | Euro R16 rate |
|---|---|
| Extra time rate | 33% (8/24) |
| PSO rate (absolute) | 13% (3/24) |
| PSO rate (among ET ties) | 38% (3/8) |
| Favourite-advances rate | ~68–72% |

**Interpretation for WC R32:** Compared to the 32-team WC R16 (which had more ET and PSOs — 38% ET rate, 21% PSO rate), the Euro R16 shows lower ET/PSO rates. This is plausible because the Euro R16 often features more lopsided ties (e.g., Spain 4-1 Georgia, Germany 2-0 Denmark in 2024). The WC R32 may sit somewhere between Euro R16 (13% PSO) and WC R16 (21% PSO). **Use 15–18% as the working PSO rate for the R32**, and **30–35% for ET rate**. Favourite-advance rate: **70%** (slightly higher than later rounds because quality gaps are still large).

These are explicitly analogized estimates, not empirical WC R32 data. The panel should weight them with appropriate uncertainty. The new third-placed qualifiers (best 8 of 12 third-place teams) will face group winners, which is more lopsided than the old R16 format.

### 2.2 Big-game temperament: clutch/choke evidence

**Argentina — Clutch:** Won 2022 WC on PSO twice. Messi's penalty converted (Final). Emi Martinez saved twice. Historical record: won PSOs vs England 1998, Netherlands 2014, Netherlands 2022, France 2022. Only loss: Germany 2006. Current spine (if intact for 2026): Martinez (GK PSO expert), Di Maria retired but De Paul, Mac Allister calm penalty takers. Rating: **strong clutch reputation, backed by evidence.**

**Germany — Clutch:** 4-0 WC PSO record, 6-1 combined. Historically the "Penalty King" nation. However, early WC exits 2018 and 2022 (group stage) undercut the narrative for recent squads. New generation under Nagelsmann showed improvement at Euro 2024 (lost to Spain in ET QF — unlucky, not a choke). Rating: **strong historical reputation; current squad not yet tested in deep WC knockout.**

**Brazil — Choke signals:** 2022 exit: led vs Croatia in ET (Neymar goal), then conceded, then missed in PSO. Rodrygo's kick saved, Marquinhos hit the post. Pre-2022 wobble: lost 7-1 to Germany 2014 (catastrophic defensive collapse). Copa America 2019 and 2021 wins show ability to close out titles. Rating: **elevated PSO vulnerability at the QF/SF level; semi-final pressure historically difficult.**

**France — Mixed:** Won 2018 WC. But: Lost 2006 WC Final PSO; Lost Euro 2020 R16 PSO vs Switzerland (massive upset as defending WC champion). Mbappé won 2022 Final PSO attempt but France lost overall. Rating: **large-tournament choke potential at the knockout level; well-documented Euro 2020 collapse (Benzema/Pogba/Mbappé era).**

**Spain — PSO choke:** WC 4-0 in PSOs is the worst major-nation record. Lost 2022 vs Morocco without scoring a single penalty (Sarabia, Soler, Busquets all missed). Also lost 2018 vs Russia (Koke, Aspas missed). Despite tiki-taka excellence, cannot execute in shootouts historically. Euro record is better (4-2 Euro total) but still mixed. Rating: **structural PSO weakness is one of the most documented in football; remains a real pricing inefficiency when Spain goes to PSO.**

**England — Improving but cautious:** Lost first 3 WC PSOs. Won 2018 vs Colombia (first ever WC PSO win). Lost Euro 2020 Final PSO (Saka, Sancho, Rashford). Won Euro 2024 QF PSO vs Switzerland (Saka converted redemption). Under current generation: improving technical preparation, but emotional fragility at high-pressure moments persists. 2024 tournament showed nervy performances vs Slovakia (equalised 95'). Rating: **historical PSO trauma reducing but not erased; individual composure variable.**

**Croatia — Clutch:** Won all 4 WC PSOs. Livakovic made 3 saves vs Japan 2022 (historic). Small squad that consistently overachieves relative to ranking. Aging core (Modric, Brozovic) — current cycle may have passed peak. Rating: **exceptional PSO record; entire squad comfortable from spot, but age of core is a concern for 2026.**

**Netherlands — PSO fragility vs Argentina specifically:** 0-2 in WC PSOs vs Argentina (2014 SF, 2022 QF). 1-3 combined Euro record. Structurally underperform in shootouts despite good players. Rating: **moderate PSO weakness; strongest signal is repeated Argentina-specific failure.**

**Portugal — PSO improving:** Euro wins 2012 (Quaresma decided), 2016 (no PSO in win, won in ET), 2024 (Diogo Costa 3 saves vs Slovenia). The Diogo Costa factor is a live skill edge — no goalkeeper has ever saved 3 in a Euro PSO before (as of 2024). WC: No PSO appearances. Rating: **Diogo Costa is a potential pricing anomaly — back Portugal in any PSO market where he starts.**

**Morocco — Limited sample but positive signals:** 2022 PSO win vs Spain (3-0 sweep, Bono saves). Compact defensive structure that favours nil-nil regulation. Historically disciplined under Regragui. Rating: **positive prior but 1 match sample; defensive organisation is their structural edge.**

**Japan — PSO vulnerability:** 0-2 WC record. Technically proficient but struggle to convert under tournament PSO pressure. Historically nail-biting group-stage exits, unable to advance past R16 in WC. Rating: **avoid as PSO pick; more likely to equalise via open play than to win a shootout.**

**Switzerland — Surprising PSO upside:** Won Euro 2020 PSO vs France (Sommer saves on Mbappé). Lost to Spain same tournament (Sommer couldn't repeat). Euro 2024: lost QF to England on PSO. Mixed. ### 2.4 Cross-cutting notes for the knockout-context lens

1. **PSO pricing inefficiency:** Spain's structural PSO weakness (4 WC losses, 1 scoring 0) is the most exploitable individual-team prior for the panel. England's improving but imperfect record creates two-way value depending on opponent.

2. **Emi Martinez / Diogo Costa edge:** Both goalkeepers have documented, tournament-tested PSO skills that standard odds rarely price fully. When either side faces a PSO, their goalkeeper's save probability should be rated above the 69% base conversion rate (implying >31% save rate per kick rather than the structural ~31%).

3. **Croatia aging spine:** Their 4-0 WC PSO record is exceptional, but if core players (Modric, Brozovic) are declining or absent, that record loses explanatory power. Flag for knockout-context lens.

4. **France Euro 2020 PSO collapse:** France went out to Switzerland in the R16 despite being defending WC champions and having Mbappé, Benzema. The lesson: tournament pedigree does not protect against individual missed penalties under pressure.

5. **The underpriced draw bucket:** 38% of all R16 ties go to ET (38% across all 6 tournaments at R16 level). The market tends to overprice outright wins, especially for heavily favoured teams — the draw/ET outcome at ~15–18 PSO rate plus ~20–22% ET-decided rates is frequently underpriced in standard 90-minute betting. This is a consistent angle for the decider.

6. **Second-half leads are not safe:** Notable instances of teams overturning second-half deficits (Belgium vs Japan 2018; Netherlands vs Argentina 2022; France vs Argentina 2022) mean that a scoreline at 75–80 minutes should not be taken as near-certain. This is especially true when the trailing side has more attacking intent (e.g., Argentina trailing = danger).
